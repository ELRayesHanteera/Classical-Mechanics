2. Intuition

What we're really asking: How fast must the satellite move so gravity bends its path into a circle? And how long does one lap take?

At radius $r$, circular motion needs centripetal acceleration $v^2/r$ pointing toward Earth's center. Gravity supplies $GM/r^2$. Balance them:

$$\frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{GM}{r^2} \quad\Rightarrow\quad v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

One orbit = circumference $2\pi r$ at speed $v$, so $T = 2\pi r/v$ and $\omega = v/r = 2\pi/T$.

Key trick: We are not given $GM$, but we are given $g$ at the surface. Since $g = GM/R^2$, we can write everything in terms of $R$, $r$, and $g$.

What if $h = 0$? Then $r = R$ and $v = \sqrt{gR} \approx 7.7$ km/s ã a familiar low-orbit speed scale.

3. Formal solution

Step 1 ã Orbital speed

$$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r} \quad\Rightarrow\quad v^2 = \frac{GM}{r}$$

Use $GM = gR^2$:

$$\boxed{v = \sqrt{\frac{gR^2}{r}} = R\sqrt{\frac{g}{r}} = R\sqrt{\frac{g}{R+h}}}$$

Plain language: Speed falls as $1/\sqrt{r}$ ã higher orbit, slower satellite.

Step 2 ã Period

$$T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{r^3}{gR^2}}$$

This is Kepler's third law for circular orbits: $T^2 \propto r^3$.

Step 3 ã Angular velocity

$$\boxed{\omega = \frac{v}{r} = \sqrt{\frac{gR^2}{r^3}} = \frac{2\pi}{T}}$$

Step 4 ã Numbers

$$v = 6.00\times10^6\sqrt{\frac{9.83}{6.72\times10^6}} \approx \mathbf{7.26\ \text{km/s}}$$

$$T = \frac{2\pi(6.72\times10^6)}{7.26\times10^3} \approx \mathbf{5.82\times10^3\ \text{s} \approx 97.0\ \text{min}}$$

$$\omega \approx \mathbf{1.08\times10^{-3}\ \text{rad/s}}$$

5. Takeaway

Given surface $g$ and $R$, circular-orbit quantities are $v = R\sqrt{g/r}$, $T = 2\pi r/v$, $\omega = v/r$. The $r^{3/2}$ scaling of $T$ is Kepler's third law in action.
